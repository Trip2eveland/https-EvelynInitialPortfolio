TYPOGRAPHY OVERVIEW

Typography is used to create visually appealing designs that enhances the appeal of once boring handwriting. Typography is a form of communication through sight. Typography, if used correctly, can improve the readability through form and beauty. It adds a cleanness for clarity and uniformity. The road to good typography is ensuring proper contrast between text and background. Choosing the right font is enhances comprehension. Hierarchy, titles and headings should stand out which can be done in various ways. Spacing goes without saying. And Alignment for that clean professional appearance.

KEY TERMS

  1. Typeface: Is a family of characters that share a common style. It is influenced by the medium and its intended audience.

  2. Font: relates to the weight, size, and style within a typeface family.

  3. Ascender Line: The guideline that marks the uppermost point of an ascender, which is the part of a lowercase letter extending above the x-height.

  4. Cap Height: The height of an uppercase letter from the baseline to the top of the letter.

  5. X-Height: The height of lowercase letters, typically measured by the lowercase "x." This measurement varies across typefaces and significantly affects readability in body text.

  6. Baseline: The invisible line on which most characters sit, providing consistency in alignment.

  7. Descender Line: The guideline indicating the lowest point of a descender, which is the portion of a lowercase letter that extends below the baseline.

  8. Serif: Small projections or "feet" at the ends of strokes in certain typefaces. Serif fonts enhance readability in long passages of text.

  9. Bracket: The curved or angled connection between a serif and the main stroke of a letter. The shape and contrast of the bracket contribute to a typeface’s style.

  10. Terminal: The end of a stroke that does not have a serif. This can be rounded, flat, or angled, depending on the typeface.

  11. Loop: A fully closed or partially closed counter at the bottom of certain lowercase letters, such as "g".

  12. Stem: The primary vertical or diagonal stroke of a letterform, providing structural support.

  13. Arm: A horizontal or upward-slanting stroke that does not connect to another stroke at one end, as seen in letters like "T" and "Y".
