What is Color Theory?

The color wheel is very useful once you learn how to use it. The color wheel was created based on a theory. Color theory which is the science of how colors work together and/or blend together to maximize the overall product of art designs and photos. It explores how well colors work and or interact with one another in art pieces. Color schemes are represented in seven (7) categories as follows: Tetradic, Analogous, triadic, achromatic, split complementary complementary and monochromatic.

Primary Color modes:

RGB:
used for digital screens (red, green, blue)
CMYK:
used for print (cyan, magenta, yellow, black)
Hexadecimal:
identifier by a number beginning with a “#” sign.

Key Terms To Know:

Primary colors:
pure and cannot be created by mixing other colors. (red, blue, yellow)
Secondary colors:
formed by mixing two primary colors. (green, violet, orange)
Tertiary colors:
mixing a primary color with a secondary color. (blue-violet, bule-green, yellow-green, yellow-orange, red-orange, red-violet)
Tints:
a hue plus white.
Shades:
is a hue plus black.
Tones:
a hue plus gray.
Neutral colors:
perceived as the absence of color, however, they contain subtle undertones.
Warm colors:
bring energy and intensity to the design, bringing emotions and excitement.
Cool colors:
add calmness and soothing atmosphere adding a peaceful and relaxing feel.
Monochromatic colors:
consists of a single hue with variations in shades and tints. Adjusting lightness and saturation for harmony. (visual cohesion, emphasis on texture and pattern, ease of design, and emotional impact)
Triadic colors:
group of three colors evenly spaced on the color wheel.
Complementary colors:
pairs of colors that when combined, cancel each other out by producing a grayscale color (like white or black) when placed next to each other, they create the strongest contrast and reinforce each other. (i.e red and green, blue and orange, yellow and purple). They sit opposite each other on the color wheel.
Split colors:
the selection of a primary color and two colors adjacent to its complement on the color wheel. The goal is avoid harsh contrasts.
Analogous:
groups of colors that are next to each other on the color wheel.
Contrasting:
the impact of combining different hues in a way that highlights their differences. Used to enhance visual impact. (creates emphasis)
Vibrancy:
is the brightness, intensity, and energy of a hue. (sets the tone)
Saturation:
the intensity and purity of a color. (used to make colors more vivid).

Hues:
the purest form of a color.
Color Scheme:
Is a defined set of colors that inables visual appearance and mental connection to the overall project.
